Over the past two decades, textual analysis has grown from a handful of exploratory studies to one of the most active empirical programmes in accounting and finance research. What began with simple word counts and general-purpose dictionaries has matured into a field that routinely deploys transformer models and large language models to extract information from corporate language.

I have just completed a comprehensive research brief that maps this entire trajectory. The report covers the full arc: from the digitisation of SEC filings that made the literature possible, through the Loughran-McDonald dictionary revolution, to the FinBERT and GPT models now pushing the frontier.

## What the brief covers

The report is organised around ten sections:

- **Data sources**: SEC filings, MD&A sections, earnings call transcripts, analyst reports, financial news, social media, patents, and regulatory communications
- **Methodological progression**: Six phases from general-purpose dictionaries to domain-specific word lists, machine learning classifiers, topic models, word embeddings, transformers, and large language models
- **Core applications**: Tone and sentiment analysis, readability and disclosure complexity, textual similarity and novelty, forward-looking and risk disclosure, deception and fraud detection, and disclosure quality measurement
- **Capital market outcomes**: How textual signals link to returns, volatility, liquidity, cost of capital, analyst forecasts, audit outcomes, credit risk, and governance
- **Validation concerns**: Measurement validity, out-of-sample testing, reproducibility, look-ahead bias, and the growing importance of construct validity as models become more complex

## Key findings

The brief draws on a compiled dataset of publication counts, method usage patterns, citation records, and application area distributions. Several findings stand out:

1. Publication volume has grown from roughly 2 papers per year in 2005 to approximately 90 in 2023.
2. Loughran and McDonald (2011) is the most-cited paper in the field, with approximately 5,700 Google Scholar citations, reflecting how widely the LM Master Dictionary is used as research infrastructure.
3. Accounting journals publish about 61 per cent of textual analysis papers, with the remaining share split across finance and general-interest journals.
4. On sentiment classification tasks, the accuracy gap between dictionary baselines (about 62 per cent) and domain-trained transformers such as FinBERT (about 88 per cent) is large enough to change the conclusions of applied studies.
5. Negative tone consistently has stronger and more persistent effects than positive tone across market outcomes, consistent with the greater credibility of unfavourable news in disclosure settings.
